SECTION 1 — THE PM MINDSET SHIFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q1. Most PMs were trained to treat engineering capacity as a scarce resource —
    the whole job was prioritizing what not to build. If that constraint is
    loosening, what actually becomes the job?

YOUR ANSWER:
Headcount was always a crutch. PMs complained about not having enough engineers, but if we're honest, the constraint was never capacity — it was clarity about what customers actually needed.

That question doesn't change when AI enters the room. Customers still have jobs to be done. We still have ways to measure whether they're succeeding. More features, shipped faster, don't help if they're the wrong features.

This is why I keep coming back to "Invent & Simplify." The cheaper it gets to build, the more important it is to cut. The PM's job in an AI-enabled world isn't to maximize output — it's to make sure we're building the right things, and that customers can actually use what we ship.



Q2. There's a version of this that sounds scary for PMs: "anyone can build now,
    so PMs are less necessary." There's another version where PMs become more
    valuable. Which do you believe, and why?

YOUR ANSWER:
Of course I'm biased — but here's the honest case:

When I was building Healthy Pathways, I kept adding features. It was just so easy. Claude would build whatever I asked for in minutes. Alpha users brought me back to reality: the app was overwhelming, and most of them couldn't get through data entry to see their analysis.

I had a real moment of doubt — is this product even useful? But the answer wasn't to gut the feature set. It was to do what a PM is actually supposed to do: figure out what customers need versus what I built. I simplified the UX significantly, and reworked the AI layer to call different models depending on how much information a user was willing to provide. More data → deeper Sonnet analysis. Less data → faster Haiku result. The customer experience got dramatically better without removing core value.

That judgment call — what to simplify, what to keep, where the real friction is — doesn't come from shipping faster. It comes from staying honest about what customers are actually experiencing. More people shipping more code doesn't produce that. Good PMs do.

Q3. You've now built two apps yourself using AI tools. What's one assumption
    you held as a PM — something you confidently told engineers — that turned
    out to be wrong once you were the one doing the building?

YOUR ANSWER:
I assumed a PM could design a product by thinking through the happy path and a handful of edge cases. Wrong.

When building MB Connect, I ran into a situation where unauthenticated users couldn't discover available merit badge classes. It looked like a UX bug. It was actually a database permissions issue — the Supabase Row Level Security policies hadn't been written to account for the guest state.

I had known that guest access would matter. I just never wrote it down clearly enough to catch the gap before it hit me in implementation.

That's the thing: as a PM, I never personally felt the cost of my own vagueness. I'd write an ambiguous spec, hand it off, and engineers would quietly absorb the rework cost while I was already two features ahead. Building this app, I was the one who had to stop, dig into the database, fix the policies, and retest. I felt it. That changed how I write specs.

The last paragraph is the post. "PMs don't feel the cost of their own vagueness" is the provocation — every engineer reading it will nod, and every PM will feel slightly called out. That's exactly where you want to be.

SECTION 2 — WORKING WITH ENGINEERING TEAMS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q4. The classic PM/eng dynamic: PM writes spec, engineer estimates, both
    negotiate scope. How does that dynamic change when AI can produce a working
    prototype in hours? Who holds the power in that room now?

YOUR ANSWER:
A working prototype is not a finished product — and the speed at which you can produce one is genuinely dangerous.

I ran this experiment on myself. With Healthy Pathways, I gave Claude a few paragraphs and let it run. Features appeared almost instantly. It was addictive. And I paid for it later with rework, because I'd let the tool make decisions that a PM should have made.

With MB Connect, I changed my approach entirely. I spent time upfront mapping each stakeholder — scout, counselor, leader, admin — and what problem they were trying to solve. I asked Claude to ask me questions before building anything. The output was cleaner from day one, not because the AI was smarter, but because I came in with better inputs.

That's the answer to who holds the power: whoever understands the customer problem most clearly. AI can prototype anything in hours. It can't tell you what to prototype, or whether it's the right thing to build. That's still the PM's job — and it always was.


Q5. A common PM failure mode was writing vague specs and letting engineers fill
    in the gaps. That gap-filling now happens at the prompt layer. Does that
    make the PM's job easier or does it just move where the ambiguity bites you?

YOUR ANSWER:
The ambiguity doesn't disappear, it just bites you later, and harder.

Good engineers are an underrated quality-control mechanism. The questions they ask in working sessions, frustrating as they are in the moment, force you to find the gaps in your own thinking before any code gets written. AI skips that step. It executes exactly what you described. If you described the wrong thing, you won't find out until you're looking at a working prototype of the wrong product.

PMs who relied on engineer pushback — often without realizing it — now have to build that check into their own process. That means asking harder questions of yourself upfront: What am I assuming? What states haven't I thought through? What happens when this goes wrong? AI won't ask those for you. It's on you to ask them before you prompt.


Q6. Engineers are also using AI tools now — they're shipping faster too. Does
    that compress timelines in a good way, or does it create new coordination
    problems (moving faster without alignment)?

YOUR ANSWER:
Both — and which one you get depends entirely on how aligned the team was before AI touched the keyboard.

Here's what often goes unnoticed: slow delivery was a natural forcing function for alignment. When rework cost weeks, teams were motivated to get on the same page before building. AI removes that friction. A misaligned team can now build the wrong thing three times faster — and only discover the misalignment when they're looking at a finished product that doesn't solve the right problem.

The coordination challenges aren't new. But the cost of skipping alignment conversations used to be visible in missed sprint dates. Now it shows up as polished, fast-shipped products that customers don't want. That's a harder problem to diagnose — and a more expensive one to fix.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 3 — WORKING WITH LEADERS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q7. Leaders set strategy and allocate resources partly based on what's
    feasible. If feasibility windows shrink from quarters to weeks, how should
    PMs be changing the conversations they're having with their leadership?

YOUR ANSWER:
The conversation needs to shift from "what can we build?" to "what should we build, and how will we know it worked?"

Feasibility used to be a legitimate leadership discussion — it was genuinely uncertain whether something could be built in a given timeframe. That constraint is loosening. What's not loosening is the difficulty of knowing whether the right thing got built. PMs should be redirecting leadership energy toward that question: What does customer success look like? What metrics tell us we got it right? What do we do if the first version doesn't move them?

The risk of not having this conversation: leaders start treating AI-enabled speed as a license to ship more roadmap items. More features, faster. That's exactly the wrong lesson. PMs need to be the ones making the case that speed without direction is just a faster way to build the wrong product.


Q8. Roadmaps are a communication tool as much as a planning tool. If your
    execution speed just went up 5x, does the quarterly roadmap still make
    sense? What replaces it?

YOUR ANSWER:
The quarterly cadence isn't the problem — the content is.

Most roadmaps are feature lists with dates. That format made sense when shipping a feature was a meaningful unit of work. When AI compresses build time, "we shipped the feature" tells you almost nothing. What you need to show is: what customer problem were we solving, what was our hypothesis, and did the metrics move?

Here's the constraint that keeps quarterly relevant: you can ship in a week, but you usually can't measure in a week. Metric movements — adoption, retention, task completion — need time to accumulate. A roadmap organized around outcomes and measurement windows makes more sense than one organized around feature releases. Same cadence, different content.

The artifact I'd push for: replace "Feature | Quarter | Team" with "Customer Problem | Hypothesis | Success Metric | Target Date." That's a roadmap leadership can actually hold a team accountable to.


Q9. Leaders often push PMs to "move faster." Now that faster is actually
    possible, what's the new pushback PMs need to be willing to have with
    leadership?

YOUR ANSWER:
The pushback isn't "we can't move faster." It's "let's make sure we agree on what faster is for."

The mistake leaders will make is hearing "AI accelerates delivery" and concluding "now we can do more." More features, more initiatives, more roadmap items. PMs need to be willing to say: that's not the opportunity. The opportunity is to get the right answer faster, not to get more wrong answers in the same amount of time.

In practice that means one conversation: when a leader says "can we move faster on X?", the PM's response should be "yes — and before we do, what would success look like for X, and how will we know we got there?" That question either surfaces the alignment you need, or exposes that the leader hasn't thought it through yet. Either way, you're better off knowing before the team ships.
