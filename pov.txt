PRODUCT POV — Q&A DRAFT
"Product Management in the Age of AI-Enabled Delivery"
================================================================================

Instructions: Answer each question as if speaking to another senior PM over

One concrete example beats three abstract observations.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 1 — THE PM MINDSET SHIFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q1. Most PMs were trained to treat engineering capacity as a scarce resource —
    the whole job was prioritizing what not to build. If that constraint is
    loosening, what actually becomes the job?

YOUR ANSWER:
Headcount was always a crutch. PMs complained about not having enough engineers, but if we're honest, the constraint was never capacity — it was clarity about what customers actually needed.

That question doesn't change when AI enters the room. Customers still have jobs to be done. We still have ways to measure whether they're succeeding. More features, shipped faster, don't help if they're the wrong features.

This is why I keep coming back to "Invent & Simplify." The cheaper it gets to build, the more important it is to cut. The PM's job in an AI-enabled world isn't to maximize output — it's to make sure we're building the right things, and that customers can actually use what we ship.



Q2. There's a version of this that sounds scary for PMs: "anyone can build now,
    so PMs are less necessary." There's another version where PMs become more
    valuable. Which do you believe, and why?

YOUR ANSWER:
Of course I'm biased — but here's the honest case:

When I was building Healthy Pathways, I kept adding features. It was just so easy. Claude would build whatever I asked for in minutes. Alpha users brought me back to reality: the app was overwhelming, and most of them couldn't get through data entry to see their analysis.

I had a real moment of doubt — is this product even useful? But the answer wasn't to gut the feature set. It was to do what a PM is actually supposed to do: figure out what customers need versus what I built. I simplified the UX significantly, and reworked the AI layer to call different models depending on how much information a user was willing to provide. More data → deeper Sonnet analysis. Less data → faster Haiku result. The customer experience got dramatically better without removing core value.

That judgment call — what to simplify, what to keep, where the real friction is — doesn't come from shipping faster. It comes from staying honest about what customers are actually experiencing. More people shipping more code doesn't produce that. Good PMs do.

Q3. You've now built two apps yourself using AI tools. What's one assumption
    you held as a PM — something you confidently told engineers — that turned
    out to be wrong once you were the one doing the building?

YOUR ANSWER:
I assumed a PM could design a product by thinking through the happy path and a handful of edge cases. Wrong.

When building MB Connect, I ran into a situation where unauthenticated users couldn't discover available merit badge classes. It looked like a UX bug. It was actually a database permissions issue — the Supabase Row Level Security policies hadn't been written to account for the guest state.

I had known that guest access would matter. I just never wrote it down clearly enough to catch the gap before it hit me in implementation.

That's the thing: as a PM, I never personally felt the cost of my own vagueness. I'd write an ambiguous spec, hand it off, and engineers would quietly absorb the rework cost while I was already two features ahead. Building this app, I was the one who had to stop, dig into the database, fix the policies, and retest. I felt it. That changed how I write specs.

The last paragraph is the post. "PMs don't feel the cost of their own vagueness" is the provocation — every engineer reading it will nod, and every PM will feel slightly called out. That's exactly where you want to be.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 2 — WORKING WITH ENGINEERING TEAMS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q4. The classic PM/eng dynamic: PM writes spec, engineer estimates, both
    negotiate scope. How does that dynamic change when AI can produce a working
    prototype in hours? Who holds the power in that room now?

YOUR ANSWER:
A working prototype is not a finished product — and the speed at which you can produce one is genuinely dangerous.

I ran this experiment on myself. With Healthy Pathways, I gave Claude a few paragraphs and let it run. Features appeared almost instantly. It was addictive. And I paid for it later with rework, because I'd let the tool make decisions that a PM should have made.

With MB Connect, I changed my approach entirely. I spent time upfront mapping each stakeholder — scout, counselor, leader, admin — and what problem they were trying to solve. I asked Claude to ask me questions before building anything. The output was cleaner from day one, not because the AI was smarter, but because I came in with better inputs.

That's the answer to who holds the power: whoever understands the customer problem most clearly. AI can prototype anything in hours. It can't tell you what to prototype, or whether it's the right thing to build. That's still the PM's job — and it always was.


Q5. A common PM failure mode was writing vague specs and letting engineers fill
    in the gaps. That gap-filling now happens at the prompt layer. Does that
    make the PM's job easier or does it just move where the ambiguity bites you?

YOUR ANSWER:
The ambiguity doesn't disappear, it just bites you later, and harder.

Good engineers are an underrated quality-control mechanism. The questions they ask in working sessions, frustrating as they are in the moment, force you to find the gaps in your own thinking before any code gets written. AI skips that step. It executes exactly what you described. If you described the wrong thing, you won't find out until you're looking at a working prototype of the wrong product.

PMs who relied on engineer pushback — often without realizing it — now have to build that check into their own process. That means asking harder questions of yourself upfront: What am I assuming? What states haven't I thought through? What happens when this goes wrong? AI won't ask those for you. It's on you to ask them before you prompt.


Q6. Engineers are also using AI tools now — they're shipping faster too. Does
    that compress timelines in a good way, or does it create new coordination
    problems (moving faster without alignment)?

YOUR ANSWER:
Both — and which one you get depends entirely on how aligned the team was before AI touched the keyboard.

Here's what often goes unnoticed: slow delivery was a natural forcing function for alignment. When rework cost weeks, teams were motivated to get on the same page before building. AI removes that friction. A misaligned team can now build the wrong thing three times faster — and only discover the misalignment when they're looking at a finished product that doesn't solve the right problem.

The coordination challenges aren't new. But the cost of skipping alignment conversations used to be visible in missed sprint dates. Now it shows up as polished, fast-shipped products that customers don't want. That's a harder problem to diagnose — and a more expensive one to fix.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 3 — WORKING WITH LEADERS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q7. Leaders set strategy and allocate resources partly based on what's
    feasible. If feasibility windows shrink from quarters to weeks, how should
    PMs be changing the conversations they're having with their leadership?

YOUR ANSWER:
The conversation needs to shift from "what can we build?" to "what should we build, and how will we know it worked?"

Feasibility used to be a legitimate leadership discussion — it was genuinely uncertain whether something could be built in a given timeframe. That constraint is loosening. What's not loosening is the difficulty of knowing whether the right thing got built. PMs should be redirecting leadership energy toward that question: What does customer success look like? What metrics tell us we got it right? What do we do if the first version doesn't move them?

The risk of not having this conversation: leaders start treating AI-enabled speed as a license to ship more roadmap items. More features, faster. That's exactly the wrong lesson. PMs need to be the ones making the case that speed without direction is just a faster way to build the wrong product.


Q8. Roadmaps are a communication tool as much as a planning tool. If your
    execution speed just went up 5x, does the quarterly roadmap still make
    sense? What replaces it?

YOUR ANSWER:
The quarterly cadence isn't the problem — the content is.

Most roadmaps are feature lists with dates. That format made sense when shipping a feature was a meaningful unit of work. When AI compresses build time, "we shipped the feature" tells you almost nothing. What you need to show is: what customer problem were we solving, what was our hypothesis, and did the metrics move?

Here's the constraint that keeps quarterly relevant: you can ship in a week, but you usually can't measure in a week. Metric movements — adoption, retention, task completion — need time to accumulate. A roadmap organized around outcomes and measurement windows makes more sense than one organized around feature releases. Same cadence, different content.

The artifact I'd push for: replace "Feature | Quarter | Team" with "Customer Problem | Hypothesis | Success Metric | Target Date." That's a roadmap leadership can actually hold a team accountable to.


Q9. Leaders often push PMs to "move faster." Now that faster is actually
    possible, what's the new pushback PMs need to be willing to have with
    leadership?

YOUR ANSWER:
The pushback isn't "we can't move faster." It's "let's make sure we agree on what faster is for."

The mistake leaders will make is hearing "AI accelerates delivery" and concluding "now we can do more." More features, more initiatives, more roadmap items. PMs need to be willing to say: that's not the opportunity. The opportunity is to get the right answer faster, not to get more wrong answers in the same amount of time.

In practice that means one conversation: when a leader says "can we move faster on X?", the PM's response should be "yes — and before we do, what would success look like for X, and how will we know we got there?" That question either surfaces the alignment you need, or exposes that the leader hasn't thought it through yet. Either way, you're better off knowing before the team ships.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 4 — THE PM'S TOOLKIT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q10. Walk me through a week in your life as a PM before AI tools were a real
     factor. Now walk me through what that same week looks like today, or what
     you think it should look like. What's different?

YOUR ANSWER:
Before: roughly a third writing narrative PRDs and requirements docs, a third in weekly status and release readiness reviews, a third in working sessions with engineers debugging spec gaps.

The biggest shift: status reviews are shrinking. When AI accelerates delivery, there's less to report on — things either shipped or they didn't. That time is moving toward sharper upfront requirements work: longer Q&A sessions to stress-test assumptions before anything gets built.

But here's the honest part: that recovered time isn't going where it should. It's not going to customer outcomes — to sitting with users, reviewing metrics, asking whether what we shipped actually moved anything. It's getting absorbed by the next feature, the next spec, the next planning cycle. The AI-enabled PM week should look like: a third on customer and outcomes research, a third on precise requirements, a third on reviewing what shipped and what it did. I'm not there yet. Most PMs aren't.

Comment from Claude: The last paragraph is a LinkedIn post by itself. "Most PMs aren't there yet" invites every reader to either agree (and share) or push back (and engage).

Q11. Which traditional PM tool (PRD, user story map, roadmap, sprint planning,
     stakeholder review) has changed the most in an AI-first workflow, and how?

YOUR ANSWER:
Most PMs thought of the PRD as a communication tool. It was actually a thinking tool. Writing in clear narrative forced you to complete your own thoughts: commit to language, finish the sentence, resolve the ambiguity before moving on. Prompting skips that. You can stay vague, iterate, and course-correct later — and it still feels productive because something is getting built.

The trap: PMs are starting to skip the PRD because the iterative prompting process feels like a substitute. It isn't. What you lose is the forcing function — the moment where you had to decide, in writing, what success looks like before any code existed.

The version I'd push for: write the PRD first, then use AI to argue against it. Ask it to find the gaps, the edge cases, the unstated assumptions. That combination — structured human thinking stress-tested by AI — is better than either one alone.

I saw this play out directly. With Healthy Pathways, I prompted with features I wanted to build. They appeared fast — and I had to undo much of it later because they weren't hitting real user needs. With MB Connect, I started with problems: what scouts, counselors, and leaders were struggling with. The iteration stayed focused on stakeholder needs before a single feature was named. By the time anything got built, I had a detailed narrative of what we were solving and for whom. Not a formal PRD — but the same thinking, produced through problem-first prompting.

Q12. Are there any PM skills or activities that AI tools have made genuinely
     worse — things you relied on that now produce lower quality output or
     create false confidence?

YOUR ANSWER:
The Amazon PRFAQ — the working-backwards artifact that forces you to imagine the product on launch day and anticipate every stakeholder question before a line of code is written. The FAQ section in particular is brutal in the best way: it forces you to articulate why a customer would actually want this, not just what it does.

AI breaks that forcing function in a subtle way. It asks clarifying questions — but about implementation, not value. "What should happen when the user clicks X?" not "Why would a user want to click X at all?" The distinction matters. You can have a very detailed, very well-implemented product that solves a problem nobody has. AI will help you build it without ever asking whether you should.

The false confidence is the real problem. After a long prompting session, it genuinely feels like you've done the hard thinking. You haven't — you've done the scoping. The hard thinking is whether this is worth building at all.

Fix: explicitly prompt AI to challenge the value proposition before touching implementation. "What assumptions am I making? Why would a customer want this? What's the simpler version?" Without that instruction, you'll keep building features. With it, you might build value.

SECTION 5 — EXPERIMENTATION WHEN DEV COST IS NEAR ZERO
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q13. Traditional A/B testing doctrine says: form a hypothesis, build the
     minimum test, measure, decide. If building is cheap, does the "minimum"
     part of that still matter? What changes about how you experiment?

YOUR ANSWER:
The rigor doesn't change. If you can't define what success looks like for a button change, you definitely can't define it for an entire flow.

What does change is the scope of what's testable. In a world where building is expensive, you test small increments — a headline, a button, a modal. When building is cheap, you can run experiments at a higher level of abstraction: does this entire onboarding flow convert better than that one? Does a guided assessment outperform a blank form? You're not just testing UI variants anymore, you're testing approaches.

That's genuinely valuable, but it raises the stakes on hypothesis quality. Bigger experiments are harder to interpret when they fail. "The flow didn't work" is less actionable than "the second step caused drop-off." "Minimum viable experiment" doesn't go away, it just stops being about how much you build and starts being about how much you can actually learn from the result.

Q14. "Fail fast" has been PM doctrine for a decade. What's the difference
     between failing fast because you validated a hypothesis and failing fast
     because you just threw something at the wall? Does cheap development blur
     that line?

YOUR ANSWER:
"Fail fast" was always a misunderstood term. The goal was never to fail, it was to learn quickly. "Understand fast" is more accurate.

Cheap development blurs that line in a specific way: when building is easy, you can ship without thinking, call it an experiment, and convince yourself you're moving fast. But if you never formed a hypothesis, you can't interpret the result. You spent less time to learn nothing. That's not failing fast — that's just shipping.

The discipline doesn't change: know what you're testing, know what signal you're looking for, and know in advance what result would cause you to change direction. Failing because you didn't get signal means your experiment was underpowered or your hypothesis was untestable. Failing because the signal clearly said "no" means you learned something. Only one of those moves you forward.

Cheap dev increases the temptation to skip that upfront work. The PM's job is to resist that — and to make sure "we shipped it to see what happens" never passes for an experiment.

Q15. If you can run 10x more experiments, you also generate 10x more signal —
     and noise. How do PMs avoid getting lost in a sea of inconclusive results?

YOUR ANSWER:
More experiments doesn't automatically mean more learning. It can mean less, if you're not selective about what you test.

The answer to avoiding inconclusive results isn't running experiments more rigorously, it's choosing experiments more carefully. Before running anything, ask: if this experiment returns a clear result, would it change what we build next? If the answer is "not really," the experiment isn't worth running. PMs should be ranking experiments by expected information value, not by how cheap they are to build.

Running concurrent experiments presents another problem which grows as it becomes easier to push experiments to customers. When multiple experiments run at the same time across the same users, you lose the ability to attribute outcomes cleanly. A user who saw both Experiment A and Experiment B doesn't tell you which one drove the result. Higher experiment velocity makes this worse. The fix is to sequence experiments where possible, or ensure they're designed to be truly independent: different user segments, different surfaces, different metrics.

The process doesn't change with volume. The stakes of running it poorly just go up.

SECTION 6 — INVENT & SIMPLIFY IN A WORLD WHERE BUILDING IS EASY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q16. Amazon's "Invent & Simplify" principle says the bias should be toward
     finding simpler ways to solve hard problems, not just adding features.
     You've lived on both sides — managing teams and building yourself. Which
     environment makes it harder to simplify, and why?

YOUR ANSWER:
The AI-enabled workflow — and it's not close.

When managing teams, simplification was partly enforced by constraint. Engineers pushed back on scope. Estimates forced trade-offs. Leaders questioned ROI. You couldn't just add a feature because you thought of it — someone would ask why, and that friction was productive.

Building with AI, none of that friction exists. I experienced it directly with Healthy Pathways: every feature I imagined got built. There was no one to say "is this actually necessary?" The app grew until users told me it was too much — and by then I'd already built the complexity in.

In a traditional environment, the organization does some of the simplification work for you. In an AI-enabled environment, it's entirely on the PM. The discipline has to be internal: a personal rule to ask "what can I remove?" before asking "what should I add?" That question doesn't come naturally. You have to build the habit deliberately — or build it into how you prompt.


Q17. When building is cheap, the temptation to add one more feature is
     constant. What's the discipline that prevents that? Is it a process, a
     principle, or just personal taste?
It's a process guided by principle and the principle is a single question: does this add value beyond what the product already delivers?

There's a feature sitting in my Healthy Pathways backlog right now called "Prep for a Doctor's Visit." The idea: a user with an upcoming appointment could use the dashboard to prepare their questions and test requests in advance. It would take a day to build.

I haven't built it. The reason: the health assessment already generates a one-page summary with exactly those things: questions to raise with your doctor and blood tests to request. Adding a separate "prep" entry point doesn't create new value, it creates a second path to the same destination. The risk of shipping it anyway is a more cluttered product that makes users think they're missing something when they're not.

In a world where building is cheap, "not yet" has to be a deliberate answer, not a default. The discipline is holding a feature in limbo until you can answer why it's better than what you already have — not just different.

Q18. Give me an example — from either Healthy Pathways or MB Connect — where
     you caught yourself over-building and had to cut something you'd already
     shipped or half-built. What made you cut it?

YOUR ANSWER:
The most counterintuitive thing I did: added backend complexity to reduce frontend complexity.

Healthy Pathways launched as a 5-step assessment. Alpha feedback showed two clear drop-offs: most users stopped after step one (demographics), and those who continued stalled at biomarker entry. The funnel was losing people before they ever saw an analysis.

The obvious move was to cut steps. But cutting would have gutted the product - a shallow health assessment isn't useful. So instead I changed what users experienced, not what the product could do. I simplified the UX to reduce the sense of being overwhelmed, and reworked the AI layer to deliver meaningful analysis at any level of data entered — routing lighter inputs to Claude Haiku for a faster result, richer inputs to Claude Sonnet for depth.

That backend rework required building new logic I hadn't planned for. But it let me keep the full value of the product while eliminating the friction that was stopping users from reaching it. Sometimes simplifying the experience means the opposite of simplifying the code.

SECTION 7 — THE BIGGER PICTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q19. There's a version of AI-enabled delivery that democratizes product
     building — more people can make things. There's another version where it
     just lets the same companies ship more features faster. Which do you think
     actually happens, and what's the PM's role in which version wins?

YOUR ANSWER:
The argument shouldn't be about making more things or shipping them faster. It's about shipping value. The individuals who build their own tools will mostly solve their own problems well. What they'll do is raise the floor on user expectations: when someone can one-click-build a passable solution to their problem, the bar for what a company needs to ship to be worth using goes up.

That's actually the most important thing for PMs right now. The threat isn't that individuals will out-ship companies. It's that the gap between "someone built this in a weekend" and "this is worth paying for" is collapsing. The PM's job becomes sharper, not broader: you're not managing delivery speed, you're defending why what you're building is worth building at all.

The companies that win won't be the ones who ship the most. They'll be the ones who ship things that individual builders can't; things that require scale, trust, integrations, institutional knowledge, or deep domain expertise. That's where PMs should be spending their judgment.

Q20. What's the one thing you wish someone had told you before you started
     building with AI tools — not about the tools themselves, but about what
     it would change about how you think as a PM?

YOUR ANSWER:
The thing that surprised me wasn't what stayed important, it was how quickly I stopped acting like it was.

PRDs, hypothesis-driven experimentation, building for customer value over feature count: none of that changes. AI reduces friction at every stage of delivery, but the fundamentals of good product thinking are just as load-bearing as they've always been.

What I didn't expect was how seductive the speed would be. When you can build fast, slowing down to ask "should we build this at all?" feels like a drag. It isn't - it's the job. The PMs who lose the thread in an AI-enabled world won't fail because they don't know the principles. They'll fail because speed made it easy to stop applying them. You end up shipping more. You don't end up shipping better.
