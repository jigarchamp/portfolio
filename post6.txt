"The Thing That Surprised Me Wasn't What Changed"

DRAFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Claude hit its usage limits. I had to wait for them to reset.

So I did what any productive person does during downtime: I started writing
notes. A few lines about what I wanted to fix in Healthy Pathways. Things
that weren't quite right. Gaps I'd noticed but hadn't addressed.

By the time the limit reset, I had paragraphs.

Not a list of improvements. A list of things I hadn't thought through
properly in the first place. I'd been building for days and the writing
revealed something the building had hidden: I was fixing the product I'd
made instead of asking whether I'd made the right product.

Fixing things piece by piece might have made the app function better.
It wouldn't have made it more valuable.

[Healthy Pathways: https://healthy-pathways.vercel.app/]

The forced pause did what discipline should have done from the start.

That's the thing about building with AI tools. The speed is real and it's
seductive. You can go from an idea to a working feature in a few hours, and
that feeling — of watching something appear — makes it very easy to keep
going without stopping to ask whether you're going in the right direction.
The next prompt is always right there. The next feature is always one
conversation away.

The discipline that's actually hard isn't technical. It's knowing when to
stop building and start thinking.

I felt this most clearly in how I approached Healthy Pathways at the
beginning. My starting point was expert knowledge: South Asians should
monitor their A1C, LP(a), and Apo(b) markers. That's medically accurate.
It's also completely backwards as a product premise.

Those markers are answers for people who have already asked the right
questions. My users hadn't gotten there yet. They weren't starting from
"I need to know my LP(a)." They were starting from "I'm middle aged, not very active, and
I don't know what to ask my doctor." I was handing them answers to questions
they hadn't thought to ask.

It took many iterations and more than a few forced cooling-down sessions
before I stopped trying to teach users about health and started trying to
understand what they were actually worried about. The app I should have
built was always about their questions, not my answers.

If I had spent the first hour on that — walking through what customers
actually experience when they think about their health — I would have saved
tons of rework.

I started this whole exercise because I was BSing my way through AI
questions in interviews and I knew it. What I didn't expect was what I'd
actually learn.

The thing that surprised me wasn't what stayed important. It was how quickly
I stopped acting like it was important.

PRDs, hypothesis-driven experimentation, building for customer value over
feature count: none of that changes. AI reduces friction at every stage of
delivery, but the fundamentals of good product thinking are just as
load-bearing as they've always been.

What I didn't expect was how seductive the speed would be. When you can
build fast, slowing down to ask "should we build this at all?" feels like
a drag. It isn't. It's the job.

The PMs who lose the thread in an AI-enabled world won't fail because they
don't know the principles. They'll fail because speed made it easy to stop
applying them.

You end up shipping more. You don't end up shipping better.

What are you going to think through before you start your next AI-assisted
build — and how long are you going to give yourself to think it through?
