Cheap Building Doesn't Change
What Makes an Experiment Real

Jigar Champaneria  ·  March 2026  ·  4 min read  ·  ← Read Post 4 first

When I shipped the rules-based assessment for Healthy Pathways, I had a clear goal: make things fast and simple for new users.

What I didn't have was a hypothesis about value. I was optimizing for speed without asking what a new user would actually do with the result. The feedback told me: the assessments lacked depth. I redesigned around Claude Haiku and got both speed and depth by letting users answer a few more questions to shape the output.

But here's what I got wrong before I got it right: I originally framed it as speed versus depth, as if they were opposites. They weren't. The real question I hadn't asked was: what would make a new user feel this was worth their time? That's a hypothesis. "Let's ship something fast" is not.

I've made the same mistake in a professional setting and the cost was harder to measure. At Amazon, we made a round of improvements to an ads carousel on the Appstore homescreen. The changes worked well in testing. When we pushed to production, the ads performed better than before. But there was no meaningful difference compared to ads placed elsewhere in the UI, and we couldn't isolate what was driving the lift.

We stuck with the changes and moved on to a longer-term rebuild. To this day, I don't know if that work was a genuine win or just productive-looking busy work. We shipped without defining what a clear result would mean, and so we couldn't interpret the result we got.

That's the failure mode cheap building accelerates. When a feature takes a sprint to build, teams are willing to do the hypothesis work first. When it takes a day, "let's just ship it and see" starts to feel like a reasonable plan. It isn't. You can't learn from a result you weren't set up to interpret.

The discipline I've landed on: before running anything, ask one question. If this experiment returns a clear result, would it change what we build next? If the answer is "not really," the experiment isn't worth running regardless of how cheap it is to build.

I had to apply that discipline during the skills integration project. Customer interviews surfaced a lot of feedback, but when I worked through it, the meaningful insights pointed to flows upstream and downstream from the Appstore itself. The concerns specific to what we owned were real but peripheral. The real customer benefit would happen elsewhere.

So instead of drafting revised requirements and testing demos in Appstore, I redirected the work to product colleagues in other parts of the experience. The experiment we almost ran wouldn't have produced signal that would change the Appstore roadmap. Running it would have felt like progress. It would have been noise.

"Fail fast" was never about failing. It was always about learning quickly. The shortcut isn't skipping the hypothesis. The shortcut is knowing in advance exactly what result would change your next decision.

Have you ever looked at experiment results and realized you couldn't tell what they meant because you hadn't decided in advance what they would mean?
