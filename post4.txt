DRAFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A year after a product launched in Japan, I was brought in to figure out what
more we could build on top of it.

I left asking why we built it at all.

The feature had been approved based on optimistic adoption projections and
competitive parity. Competitors offered something similar, the idea felt right,
and a lot of people signed up for work that produced a clear deliverable. What
it didn't produce was a clear answer to why customers would care. By the time
I got there, the data told the story: adoption was flat, and the customers the
product was built for hadn't asked for it.

This wasn't a delivery failure. The team shipped on time. It was a
clarity failure, upstream of everything else.

I thought about that project a lot while I was building with AI tools this
year. The problem it represents isn't new. But in a world where features take
days instead of quarters to ship, it's easier than ever to repeat it. Speed
without a value thesis just means arriving at the wrong place faster.

The most important leadership conversation a PM can have right now isn't about
velocity. It's about direction.

I learned that the hard way on a different project. We were integrating Alexa
skills into the Appstore, and for a stretch of weeks, the product and
technical complexity kept growing. More logic, more caveats, more edge cases.
The team was moving, but something felt off.

I went to my Principal. Not with a list of grievances, and not with a proposed
solution. I said: the complexity we're seeing is a signal that something isn't
right at the bigger picture level. I don't know what the answer is, but I
don't think the answer is to keep pushing.

That conversation led to a product simplification. The technical complexity
followed. And when we finally shipped, the stakeholders who'd been waiting
told us the simplified version addressed doubts they'd had all along but
hadn't raised. We hadn't just removed friction from the build. We'd removed
friction from the product.

Sometimes the path to delivery isn't pushing through. It's pausing long enough
to ask whether the direction is right.

I'll be honest about where I fall short on this: I know the right format for
a roadmap. Problem statement. Hypothesis. Success metric. Target date. I've
used it at the PRFAQ stage and in QBRs. But between those two moments, when
the team is heads-down and delivery pressure is real, I fall back to the
feature list like everyone else. The bullet points of shipped things feel good.
They're easy to report. They're also the wrong unit of measurement for whether
you're actually moving anywhere.

AI doesn't change that failure mode. It just makes it cheaper to repeat.

When your leader asks you to move faster, the right response isn't "we can't"
or even "we will." It's: let's make sure we agree on what faster is for. What
does success look like? How will we know we got there? What do we do if the
first version doesn't move the needle? And even if you don't know the answers, just asking those questions is a powerful way to get clarity.

The questions either surface the alignment you need, or expose that nobody
has thought it through yet. Either way, you're better off knowing before the
team ships.

When did you last push your team to move faster and already know what you were
moving toward?

NOTES FOR REVISION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HOOK TEST: "A year after a product launched in Japan, I was brought in to
figure out what more we could build. I left asking why we built it at all."
Works before "see more." Subverts the expected "here's what I learned by
building more" setup. Forces the "what happened?" question immediately.

THE JAPAN STORY: Keep the "not quite groupthink" nuance. The more precise
framing is: people made individually rational decisions (competitor parity,
clear deliverable) that added up to a collectively bad outcome. That's more
honest and more interesting than "leadership made a bad call."

THE PRINCIPAL CONVERSATION: The key move is "I didn't go in with grievances,
I went in with a bigger picture question." That's the tactical PM advice
buried in the story. Readers who've been afraid to raise concerns with
leadership will use this framing.

THE ROADMAP ADMISSION: "I fall back to the feature list like everyone else"
is the self-aware/brave beat this post needs. Without it, the post reads as
advice from someone who has it all figured out. With it, the post reads as
hard-won honesty.

ENDING QUESTION: "When did you last push your team to move faster and already
know what you were moving toward?" Directed at leaders and senior PMs. Will
land differently depending on which side of the conversation you're usually on.
