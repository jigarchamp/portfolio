Post 2 of 6 — AI Product Management

Engineers Absorbed Your Vagueness.
AI Won't.

Jigar Champaneria  ·  March 2026  ·  4 min read  ·  ← Read Post 1 first

I logged out of MB Connect to test the guest experience.

The dashboard was empty. Not broken...empty. No merit badges. No classes. No way for an unauthenticated user to discover anything.

I had written that scouts should be able to view merit badges. I hadn't written anything about what unauthenticated users could see. I assumed it was obvious. It wasn't. And Claude had no way to know what I meant because I hadn't finished the thought myself.

Fixing it forced me to think through something I'd never explicitly done: not just what unauthenticated users should see, but what they shouldn't. The positive flow and the negative flow. I had vaguely described one and hadn't even touched upon the other.

This isn't a new problem. It's a PM problem that AI makes impossible to hide.

At Amazon, my first project involved surfacing Alexa skills in Appstore discovery flows. My requirement: "skills should be discoverable alongside apps when users are logged in." The design mockups looked great. The flows made sense. I felt good about it.

Then the senior engineer came back with a prototype.

Skills I expected to see weren't showing up except through explicit search. Browse performance was bad. What followed was a product/engineering deep dive — the kind where engineers explain the real constraints and a PM quietly realizes how much they left out of the original requirement. I went back and rewrote the spec from scratch: positive flows, negative flows, open questions included.

I didn't write a better spec because I was smarter. I wrote it because an engineer showed me what I'd missed.

That's what I didn't appreciate until I built something myself: good engineers are an underrated quality-control mechanism. The questions they ask in working sessions, frustrating as they are in the moment, force you to find the gaps in your own thinking before any code gets written.

AI skips that step entirely. It executes what you described. If you described the wrong thing, you won't find out until you're looking at a working prototype of the wrong product.

When I found that empty dashboard, I felt dumb. The parallel to Amazon was obvious and I couldn't shake it. I had built something based on incomplete thinking and the back and forth friction I was used to with real engineering teams was nowhere to be found.

I had only myself, and a non-sentient chatbot, to blame.

What I do differently now: before I write a single prompt, I define both what should happen and what shouldn't. Guest vs. authenticated. Logged in vs. logged out. Known user vs. unknown user. It's the same discipline good specs always required, I just finally felt the cost of skipping it.

If you're an engineer reading this: how many times have you quietly absorbed a PM's vagueness and they never knew?
