Simplifying for Users Means
Complicating the Code

Jigar Champaneria  ·  March 2026  ·  4 min read  ·  ← Read Post 2 first

I built a rules-based health analysis into Healthy Pathways specifically to simplify things for new users. No AI model, no wait time, instant results.

An alpha tester looked at it and told me it felt no different than Googling his age, weight, and ethnicity.

He was right. I tore it down.

What came next was messier than I expected. I replaced the rules-based logic with Claude Sonnet, the same model I use for authenticated users. It was too slow and too verbose. It didn't feel like a simpler experience, just a wordier one. So I went back and researched the model options more carefully. Claude Haiku turned out to be the right fit: faster, cheaper, and with enough depth to actually give a new user something worth reading.

The final result was more backend complexity than I'd started with. I'd torn down a "simple" solution, iterated twice, and landed on something harder to build that was genuinely simpler to use.

I learned this from a conversation, not a dashboard. I don't have analytics wired up yet, so when alpha users told me something wasn't clicking, I had to rely on what they said, not what the numbers showed. I'm a data-driven PM by default, and I had to actively resist the urge to dismiss the feedback as "they just don't get it." They got it fine. Something I'd built wasn't earning their attention, and that was on me.

The harder lesson came later. I was talking to my uncle. He hadn't tried the app, he just knew I was building it. He asked me: "Should I get an echocardiogram?"

He wasn't asking for a feature. He wasn't asking for a prep checklist or a guided flow. He wanted a clear answer to a specific question.

I realized the app would already give him that. The one-page summary it generates includes exactly that kind of recommendation: what to ask for, what to flag, what to monitor. The problem wasn't a missing feature. The problem was that the answer was buried well enough that a new user couldn't find it.

Around the same time, alpha users were asking me to build more around doctor visits: more questions, more tools, more ways to prepare. I understood the request. I said no to most of it. Not because the idea was bad, but because building another path to the same destination would clutter the product and make users feel like they were missing something when they weren't.

The discipline isn't about building less. It's about asking what you already have before you reach for something new.

In a world where building is cheap, that question gets harder to ask. The next feature is always one prompt away. The right answer is almost always to make what exists better.

What feature have you built recently that you later realized was already covered by something you'd shipped six months ago?
