================================================================================
POST 5 — EXPERIMENTS WITHOUT HYPOTHESES ARE JUST SHIPPING
"Cheap Building Doesn't Change What Makes an Experiment Real"
================================================================================

INTENDED AUDIENCE
PMs and growth teams who run experiments, data scientists who've reviewed
underpowered tests, anyone who's been in a post-launch review where nobody
could interpret the results. "Fail fast" advocates will push back — the post
reframes it as "learn fast" without being confrontational.

ANTICIPATED COMMENTS/QUESTIONS TO PREEMPT IN THE TEXT:
- "Isn't this just fail fast?" → no: fail fast means learn fast, and you
  can't learn from a result you weren't set up to interpret
- "You can always learn something from shipping" → only if you defined
  what you were trying to learn before you shipped
- "How do you know when an experiment is worth running?" → answer it
  explicitly: if a clear result wouldn't change what you build next,
  it's not worth running

NOTE: Posts 1-4 established the pattern. This post is more tactical. The
HP story ties back to Post 3 (same rules-based arc) but the lesson here is
about hypothesis, not simplification. Acknowledge the callback briefly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DRAFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When I shipped the rules-based assessment for Healthy Pathways, I had a clear
goal: make things fast and simple for new users.

What I didn't have was a hypothesis about value. I was optimizing for speed
without asking what a new user would actually do with the result. The feedback
told me: the assessments lacked depth. I redesigned around Claude Haiku and
got both speed and depth by letting users answer a few more questions to shape
the output.

But here's what I got wrong before I got it right: I originally framed it as
speed versus depth, as if they were opposites. They weren't. The real question
I hadn't asked was: what would make a new user feel this was worth their time?
That's a hypothesis. "Let's ship something fast" is not.

[Healthy Pathways: https://healthy-pathways.vercel.app/]

I've made the same mistake in a professional setting and the cost was harder
to measure. At Amazon, we made a round of improvements to an ads carousel on
the Appstore homescreen. The changes worked well in testing. When we pushed
to production, the ads performed better than before. But there was no
meaningful difference compared to ads placed elsewhere in the UI, and we
couldn't isolate what was driving the lift.

We stuck with the changes and moved on to a longer-term rebuild. To this day,
I don't know if that work was a genuine win or just productive-looking busy
work. We shipped without defining what a clear result would mean, and so we
couldn't interpret the result we got.

That's the failure mode cheap building accelerates. When a feature takes a
sprint to build, teams are willing to do the hypothesis work first. When it
takes a day, "let's just ship it and see" starts to feel like a reasonable
plan. It isn't. You can't learn from a result you weren't set up to interpret.

The discipline I've landed on: before running anything, ask one question.
If this experiment returns a clear result, would it change what we build next?
If the answer is "not really," the experiment isn't worth running regardless
of how cheap it is to build.

I had to apply that discipline during the skills integration project. Customer
interviews surfaced a lot of feedback, but when I worked through it, the
meaningful insights pointed to flows upstream and downstream from the Appstore
itself. The concerns specific to what we owned were real but peripheral. The
real customer benefit would happen elsewhere.

So instead of drafting revised requirements and testing demos in Appstore, I
redirected the work to product colleagues in other parts of the experience.
The experiment we almost ran wouldn't have produced signal that would change
the Appstore roadmap. Running it would have felt like progress. It would have
been noise.

"Fail fast" was never about failing. It was always about learning quickly.
The shortcut isn't skipping the hypothesis. The shortcut is knowing in advance
exactly what result would change your next decision.

Have you ever looked at experiment results and realized you couldn't tell what
they meant because you hadn't decided in advance what they would mean?
